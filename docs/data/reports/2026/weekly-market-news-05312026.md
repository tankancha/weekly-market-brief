### 1. Iran War — Oil Shock & Ceasefire Volatility (Impact Score: 45)

**Event Date:** May 28 (US-Iran retaliatory strikes); May 29–30 (ceasefire talks)
**Event Type:** Geopolitical / Commodity Shock
**News Source:** CNBC, CNN, Reuters — Tier 1/2

#### Event Summary

The 2026 Iran-US war — which began earlier in the year and produced a first ceasefire on April 8 — reignited this week as the US and Iran exchanged retaliatory strikes on May 28. The US Defense Secretary warned on May 29–30 that military forces were ready to resume full combat operations if needed. Concurrently, the US and Iran were "mostly agreed" on a 60-day ceasefire extension MOU, but Trump's additional demands on May 30 stalled Tehran's sign-off. The Strait of Hormuz — responsible for ~20% of global oil supply — remains well below pre-war shipping volumes despite the nominal ceasefire, creating a persistent supply bottleneck. Brent crude peaked near $112 at the height of the conflict before collapsing -20% as ceasefire momentum built.

#### Market Reaction

**Immediate (Day-of):**

- **Equities:** Mixed — energy sector supported; broader S&P held losses below -0.5% on ceasefire optimism
- **Bonds:** 10Y yield ~4.45%; inflation breakevens elevated from persistent energy pass-through
- **Commodities:** WTI $87.86, Brent $91.89 (May 29 settle) — oil -19% in May, worst month since COVID. Gold $4,541 (+1.0% on the day), YTD +43%, driven by safe-haven bid and USD weakness
- **Currencies:** DXY 98.97, -2.17% MTD; USD softened as ceasefire optimism reduced flight-to-dollar flows
- **Volatility:** VIX subdued — market pricing in ceasefire success; represents a significant contrarian risk

**Follow-Through:** Unresolved — binary outcome. Full Strait of Hormuz reopening vs. renewed escalation remain equally credible scenarios heading into June.

**Pattern Comparison:** **Inverse** on oil (market pricing in supply normalization despite active strikes — WTI -19% MTD vs typical +5-15% during active Middle East conflict). **Consistent** on gold (safe-haven + USD weakness). Oil market is pricing ceasefire with high confidence — a notable setup risk if MOU collapses.

#### Impact Assessment Detail

- **Price Impact:** Severe — WTI -19% monthly; Gold +43% YTD; DXY -2.17% MTD
- **Breadth:** Systemic — energy, precious metals, equities, bonds (via inflation), FX, EM currencies
- **Forward Significance:** Regime Change — full Hormuz reopening removes 2-3% from energy price baseline; failure risks re-spike to $100+
- **Calculated Score:** (10 × 3) × 1.5 = **45**

#### Sector-Specific Impacts

Energy E&P stocks face bearish bias if ceasefire succeeds (lower oil prices). Airlines and Consumer Discretionary benefit from sub-$90 WTI. Defense stocks (LMT, RTX, NOC) remain structurally elevated. Refiners (MPC, VLO) benefit from crack spread normalization. EM oil-importing countries (India, South Korea) see currency relief as oil falls.

#### Commodity Correlation

- **WTI/Brent:** From ~$112 peak to $87.86/$91.89; May drawdown worst since COVID-19 (April 2020)
- **Supply mechanism:** Iran accounts for ~3.5M bbl/day; Strait of Hormuz blockage removed ~4-5M bbl/day from accessible global supply at peak disruption
- **Historical precedent:** 2019 Saudi Aramco attacks: +15% single-day; this Iran war generated ~+40% peak before reversal — broader and deeper than typical Middle East shocks
- **Expected duration:** Partial Hormuz reopening likely in June under MOU; full normalization takes 3-6 months minimum given infrastructure damage to Iranian refineries and Gulf shipping logistics

### 2. FOMC Minutes Hawkish Shift + Kevin Warsh Era Begins (Impact Score: 31.5)

**Event Date:** May 15 (Warsh takes office); May 20 (FOMC April minutes released)
**Event Type:** Monetary Policy — Regime Transition
**News Source:** FederalReserve.gov, CNBC, NPR, WSJ — Tier 1

#### Event Summary

Two interconnected events mark a structural inflection in US monetary policy. Kevin Warsh was confirmed as the 17th Federal Reserve Chair by a 54-45 Senate vote — the closest in modern era, nearly entirely along party lines — and officially took office on May 15 as Powell's term expired. Warsh's stated priorities: tighter inflation discipline, streamlined Fed communication, and a more narrowly focused central bank. His first FOMC meeting is set for June 16–17. Separately, the April 28–29 FOMC minutes (released May 20) revealed a record **four dissents** — the most since 1992 — at Powell's final meeting. The minutes noted inflation "elevated and moving higher, led by energy prices," labor market "stabilized," and left the door explicitly open to rate **hikes** if conditions warrant. Market pricing for 2026 rate cuts was fully erased following the release.

#### Market Reaction

**Immediate (Day-of):**

- **Equities:** Initial selloff on hawkish read absorbed within sessions; S&P 500 ultimately logged 9th consecutive weekly gain driven by NVIDIA earnings momentum. Motley Fool flagged it as "a big shift that may upend a historically pricey stock market"
- **Bonds:** 10Y yield 4.45% (+7bps for May); 2Y yield 3.98%; yield curve still modestly inverted; market fully pricing out 2026 cuts; ~20% probability of a June hike
- **Commodities:** Gold holding near $4,541 — monetary regime uncertainty elevates gold as insurance vs unknown Warsh policy stance
- **Currencies:** DXY -2.17% MTD despite hawkish minutes — Iran war/geopolitical dynamics dominated dollar flow vs. rate differentials
- **Volatility:** VIX remained subdued; equity market proved remarkably resilient to the hawkish regime change signal

**Follow-Through:** June 16–17 Warsh FOMC is now the critical near-term catalyst for all asset classes.

**Pattern Comparison:** **Dampened** equity reaction vs historical precedent. Typically, hawkish minutes with rate hike language → S&P -0.5-1.0%. Actual: S&P +0.22% on the shortened week. AI earnings cycle narrative (NVIDIA, Rank 4) provided sufficient countervailing demand to absorb the hawkish shock.

#### Impact Assessment Detail

- **Price Impact:** Major — rate cut expectations fully erased; bond market repriced; equity multiple under latent pressure
- **Breadth:** Systemic — all asset classes affected by Fed leadership and policy direction
- **Forward Significance:** Regime Change — Warsh + record dissents = potential end of easing cycle; June 16–17 is the first real test
- **Calculated Score:** (7 × 3) × 1.5 = **31.5**

#### Sector-Specific Impacts

Utilities, REITs, and small-caps (high floating-rate debt) structurally underweight in higher-for-longer regime. Banks/Financials benefit from NIM expansion if Warsh holds or hikes. Growth/Tech faces higher theoretical discount rates but AI demand narrative overrides valuation headwinds near-term. Consumer credit quality (Capital One, Synchrony) at risk as higher rates extend borrower stress.

### 3. PCE April +3.8% YoY — 3-Year Inflation High (Impact Score: 21)

**Event Date:** May 28, 2026
**Event Type:** Economic Data — Inflation
**News Source:** BEA.gov, Benzinga, CNN Business — Tier 1/2

#### Event Summary

The Bureau of Economic Analysis released April 2026 PCE data on May 28, showing headline PCE at +3.8% YoY — the highest since May 2023 — and Core PCE at +3.3% YoY — the highest since October 2023. Both readings met expectations. The one silver lining: monthly PCE came in at +0.4%, missing the +0.5% consensus and decelerating from March's +0.7% print, suggesting monthly momentum may be plateauing. Iran war energy pass-through is driving headline; Trump tariff pass-through (104% China baseline) is driving core goods inflation. The personal saving rate collapsed to **2.6%** — the lowest since June 2022, down from 4.3% in January 2026 — signaling consumers are funding spending via drawdowns rather than income growth.

#### Market Reaction

**Immediate (Day-of):**

- **Equities:** Largely shrugged — the monthly deceleration (0.4% vs 0.5% est) was the market's focus; Memorial Day-shortened week context dampened reaction; equities broadly flat to slightly positive
- **Bonds:** 10Y yield 4.45%; FOMC minutes had already set the hawkish baseline; PCE data reinforced rather than changed rate expectations
- **Commodities:** Gold held near $4,541 — real rates at 2.06% (10Y TIPS) remain elevated but gold's geopolitical + debasement demand overrides
- **Currencies:** DXY marginally softer on the day; monthly miss on PCE slightly dollar-negative at the margin

**Follow-Through:** Data reinforces Warsh's mandate for tighter policy. No dovish pivot signaled anywhere in the data. Consumer saving rate collapse is a leading indicator of consumption contraction — watch June and July retail sales.

**Pattern Comparison:** **Dampened** equity reaction vs expected. CPI/PCE surprise at 3-year high would historically generate -0.5-1.0% S&P selloff; actual reaction near-zero, driven by (1) monthly deceleration, (2) NVIDIA/AI optimism anchoring sentiment, (3) ceasefire oil narrative suggesting headline relief ahead.

#### Impact Assessment Detail

- **Price Impact:** Major — PCE well above 2% target; complicates all rate cut scenarios; saving rate signal adds recession risk
- **Breadth:** Cross-Asset — bonds, equities (via discount rates), dollar
- **Forward Significance:** Regime Change — inflation above target + consumer balance sheet deterioration = stagflation risk structurally elevated
- **Calculated Score:** (7 × 2) × 1.5 = **21**

#### Sector-Specific Impacts

Consumer Discretionary at risk as saving rate erodes discretionary budget. Consumer Staples volume declining as elevated prices hit demand (volume-for-price trade-off deteriorating). Financial sector consumer credit (credit cards, auto loans) faces rising delinquency risk as saving buffer approaches zero. Healthcare and utilities (defensive, inelastic demand) offer relative shelter.

### 5. US-China Trade Truce & Tariff Extension (Impact Score: 10)

**Event Date:** May 12 (truce); May 14 (Trump-Xi summit); ongoing through week
**Event Type:** Geopolitical / Trade Policy
**News Source:** CNBC, Bloomberg, USCC China Bulletin — Tier 1/2

#### Event Summary

On May 12, the US and China reached a partial trade truce following Trump's April 9 tariff escalation that had lifted the US baseline tariff on Chinese imports to 104%. The Trump-Xi summit on May 14 produced three commitments: Chinese purchases of US farm products and aircraft to increase, establishment of working groups for tariff reductions on "non-strategic" goods, and USTR extension of tariff exclusions on 178 Chinese product categories until November 10. Unresolved: Chinese rare earth export restrictions and US advanced semiconductor technology controls (H20 chip rules). Trade data context: Chinese exports to the US fell -16% in Q1 2026, while China aggressively re-routed exports to Southeast Asia (+20%), Africa (+32%), and EU (+21%) — reducing bilateral leverage over time.

#### Market Reaction

**Immediate (May 12–14):**

- **Equities:** Positive — tech sector (supply chain, NVDA H20 China sales) partially relieved; S&P 500 supported; risk appetite improved
- **Bonds:** Minimal direct impact
- **Commodities:** Agricultural commodities (soybeans, corn) firmed modestly on China purchase commitments; copper stable on China demand outlook improvement
- **Currencies:** Yuan stable; DXY marginally softer on reduced geopolitical tail risk; AUD (China proxy) mildly positive
- **Volatility:** VIX declined as trade war extreme tail risk was partially removed

**Follow-Through:** Cautiously positive but fragile — rare earth/semiconductor issues remain live bilateral risks. Markets treated this as de-escalation, not resolution.

**Pattern Comparison:** **Consistent** with historical US-China truce patterns (May 2019, Phase 1 December 2019) — initial risk-on, follow-through limited by unresolved core structural issues. No regime change.

#### Impact Assessment Detail

- **Price Impact:** Moderate — prevented further escalation; primarily positive for risk sentiment rather than direct price move
- **Breadth:** Cross-Asset — equities (tech/industrials), agricultural commodities, currencies
- **Forward Significance:** Trend Confirmation (+25%) — de-escalation path credible but incomplete; rare earth/semiconductor issues remain unresolved binary risk
- **Calculated Score:** (4 × 2) × 1.25 = **10**

Rare earth-dependent sectors (defense, EV, clean energy): unresolved Chinese export restriction risk remains a structural supply chain vulnerability.

## Thematic Synthesis

### Dominant Market Narrative

**"AI-driven risk-on colliding with stagflationary macro."** Equity markets are being pulled higher by AI earnings momentum (NVIDIA's $81.6B quarter, Mag-7 $649B combined 2026 capex) and Iran war ceasefire optimism, while simultaneously under pressure from the highest inflation in 3 years, a new hawkish Fed chair, and a consumer whose savings rate has nearly halved since January. The result is a bifurcated market: large-cap AI/tech in a persistent bull run; rate-sensitive and consumer-facing sectors structurally stressed.

### Interconnected Events

- **Iran War → PCE → Fed:** The energy price shock from the Iran war (Rank 1) directly drove headline PCE to +3.8% (Rank 3), which in turn forced the FOMC into its hawkish minutes stance (Rank 2) and legitimized Warsh's inflation-fighting mandate. These three events form a single causal chain.
- **AI Capex as the Shock Absorber:** NVIDIA's $81.6B earnings and $91B guidance (Rank 4) provided the dominant risk-on counterforce that absorbed hawkish Fed signals and inflation data — explaining why the S&P 500 logged its 9th consecutive weekly gain despite deteriorating macro fundamentals.
- **Trade Truce Enables AI Exports:** The US-China truce (Rank 5) preserved NVIDIA's ability to sell H20 chips in China and removed the immediate risk of rare earth restrictions that could constrain US AI infrastructure buildout — indirectly supporting the AI demand cycle that is sustaining equity markets.

### Market Regime Assessment

**Risk Appetite:** Mixed — Risk-On for large-cap tech/AI; Risk-Off for consumer, rate-sensitive, and commodity-import-heavy sectors.
**Evidence:** VIX subdued; 9 consecutive weekly S&P gains; Nasdaq +8% MTD; Dow first-ever above 51,000 — but saving rate 2.6%, PCE at 3-year high, real 10Y yield 2.06%, record FOMC dissents.
**Sector Rotation:** AI/Tech and Semiconductors leading. Consumer Discretionary, REITs, Utilities, and Small Caps lagging. Energy sector in binary flux — ceasefire bearish for E&P; no ceasefire supportive.

### Commodity Deep Dive

- **Energy:** WTI $87.86, Brent $91.89 — down -19% in May (worst month since COVID). OPEC+ spare capacity backstops further collapse. Full Hormuz reopening could bring WTI to $75-80 range; renewed conflict risks retracing to $100+.
- **Precious Metals:** Gold $4,541/oz — YTD +43%. Drivers: (1) Iran war safe-haven bid, (2) DXY -2.17% MTD, (3) central bank buying (dedollarization theme), (4) monetary regime uncertainty under Warsh. The traditional gold/real-rate inverse relationship has decoupled — central bank structural demand is overriding the 2.06% real yield headwind.
- **Base Metals:** Copper range-bound ~$4.20-$4.50/lb. China stimulus positive offset by property sector drag. US-China trade truce a mild positive. Dr. Copper is flashing a mixed signal — not a recessionary collapse, not a boom signal.
- **Agricultural:** Soybeans and corn firmed on China purchase commitments from the trade truce. Wheat stable — Black Sea constraints linger but 2025 seasonal relief reduced war-era pressure.

### Anomalies and Surprises

1. **Equity resilience vs stagflationary data:** S&P 500 posting 9 consecutive weekly gains amid PCE at a 3-year high and hawkish Fed minutes is historically anomalous. AI earnings are masking macro deterioration.
2. **Gold-real yield decoupling:** Gold at $4,541 YTD +43% despite real yields at 2.06% — the traditional inverse relationship has decoupled. Central bank demand and geopolitical risk premiums are now primary drivers rather than interest rate differentials.
3. **Oil collapsed on active strikes:** US-Iran retaliatory strikes on May 28 failed to sustain any oil rally — May ended as oil's worst month since COVID. Markets are pricing in ceasefire success with high confidence: a significant contrarian setup if MOU fails.
